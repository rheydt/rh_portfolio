Wellesley College

The website of a prestigious liberal arts college, with over 10,000 pages and 1 million monthly page views.

Desktop view

Wellesley's homepage features a 'Daily Shot' - a large image, linked to a news story, that changes daily. A button in the corner of the image triggers a pop-over caption, and also includes links to share the news story to social media sites.

A two-part header includes icons and links for MyWellesey (the college's intranet) and the donation page, the Wellesley College logo, and a customized Google search. A floating footer (which can be collapsed for the user's whole session) provides links to popular subpages.

Beneath the Daily Shot, thumbnail images are shown for the previous 5 Daily Shot images, and display captions on rollover. Below, the page includes three columns: a list of links to sections 'especially for' various site audiences (e.g. prospective students, current students, faculty); a feed of events held at the college; and a collection of icons linking to social media sites, a tuition calculator, and a site detailing current activity for a large renovation project.

Tablet view

A different crop of the same Daily Shot image is shown for tablets. The top navigation is collapsed into a hamburger menu, and the search bar (here shown expanded) becomes a full-width bar triggered by an icon in the header. Fewer thumbnail images are shown.

Mobile view

A full-screen vertical crop of the Daily Shot image is shown for mobile, and thumbnails are eliminated. Lower page contents are displayed in a single column, instead of three.

Davis Museum

Homepage

The Davis Museum has a path-based logo image that changes for each main section of the website. Besides the top navigation, there is an icon-based nav column on the right which uses an accordion interaction to hide cluttering details such as the museum's hours and address, while still leaving the information easily accessible.

Homepage: Slideshow blocks

The body of the homepage includes a large slideshow of annotated images of the museum, and many smaller thumbnail slideshows linking to each section of the website. These slideshows are dynamically populated with images from the relevant sections of the website, and do not rotate automatically, to reduce distraction.

Artwork shuffle, Friends of Art

A section of the website dedicated to Friends of Art, individuals who support the museum through a paid membership.

The page features a Javascript animation, showing three columns of artwork (donated by Friends of Art) 'shuffling' in a slot-machine style, finally settling on 3 random images. The animation is triggered on page load, or by pressing the 'Shuffle again' link.

Recent Acquisitions: Main page

A full-screen vertical crop of the Daily Shot image is shown for mobile, and thumbnails are eliminated. Lower page contents are displayed in a single column, instead of three.

Recent Acquisitions: Artwork detail page

Clicking on an artwork's thumbnail on the main Recent Acquisition page brings you to a full image, description, and other information about the piece. A list of recent acquisitions remains to the right for easy browsing.

Course Browser

Desktop view

Google Analytics statistics showed that the Wellesley College course browser was being frequented by students on mobile devices, but this PHP web application was not responsive. Additionally, faculty teaching Humanities courses reported a decrease in enrollment over the past few years, while science and technology courses were consistently overbooked. These realizations led to a full responsive redesign, a fresh new look, and a 'Featured Courses' section to increase awareness about courses with fewer than 75% of available seats filled.

Courses are color-coded by department, and pull image(s) of the instructor(s) from the Drupal database of the college website, which link to the web profiles of each faculty member. Detailed course information is hidden, and expanded on click. Information only relevant to current students (such as the course registration number) is hidden unless a user logs into the app.

Logging into the app allows users to 'favorite' a course, which marks it with a yellow star and adds the course to the user's My Favorites page, which can then be emailed, tweeted, or Facebook shared to the user's friends or advisors.
